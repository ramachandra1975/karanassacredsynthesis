Part One · 3 Pages

Origin & Presence of the Karana

What is a Karana?

The word Karana derives from the Sanskrit root kri — to do, to act, to cause. In the profound vocabulary of classical Indian aesthetics, a Karana is neither a mere step nor a gesture in isolation. It is a complete, indivisible unit of sacred movement: a synchronised confluence of hasta (hand gesture), pada (foot movement), and sharira (body posture) that together constitute one syllable of the cosmic dance-language spoken by Nataraja, the Lord of the Dance.

Bharata Muni's Natya Shastra — the foundational treatise on performing arts composed between 200 BCE and 200 CE — enumerates precisely 108 such Karanas in its fourth chapter (Chaturthadhyaya). Each Karana is defined with architectural precision: the exact placement of the feet, the configuration of the hands, the angle of the torso, and the quality of inner intention that animates the outer form. Bharata calls them the "building blocks of all movement in the universe," a declaration that lifts dance from performance into ontology.

A Karana is therefore a body-mantra — a mantra composed not of sound alone but of integrated somatic intelligence. Just as each akshara (syllable) of a Vedic mantra holds vibrational potency, each Karana holds kinetic and spiritual potency. To perform a Karana correctly is to pronounce the universe's language through one's own living body.

Angikam bhuvanam yasya, vachikam sarva vangmayam, aaharyam chandra taradi, tam vande satvikam shivam. — Whose body is the universe, whose speech is all language, whose ornaments are the moon and stars — to that Shiva who is pure sattva, I bow.

How the Karana Originated in Natya Shastra and Vedic Ritual

The Natya Shastra identifies itself as the Fifth Veda — Panchamaveda — a revelation given by Brahma to the sage Bharata for the benefit of all beings. In this cosmic framing, the Karanas are not human inventions but divine transmissions. Brahma himself synthesised the Natya Veda from the four existing Vedas: taking pathya (words) from the Rig Veda, abhinaya (gesture and expression) from the Yajur Veda, gita (song) from the Sama Veda, and rasa (aesthetic essence) from the Atharva Veda. The Karanas therefore inherit all four Vedic streams simultaneously.

In Vedic ritual, movement was never incidental. The adhvaryu priest of the Yajur Veda performed every act of the Soma sacrifice — the measuring, pouring, offering, and circumambulating — with precisely prescribed bodily gestures, each mirroring a cosmic principle. The laying of the fire altar (agnichayana) required the priest's movements to enact the dismembered body of Prajapati being reconstituted. The body of the sacrificer was thus the body of the cosmos. The Karanas are the artistic-meditative evolution of this principle: the sacred body reconstructed not upon a sacrificial platform but upon the stage of consciousness.

The Yajur Veda's Shatapatha Brahmana explicitly speaks of the correlation between bodily posture and cosmic function. The standing body corresponds to the vertical axis of the universe (the Skambha); the extended arms to the four directions; the head to heaven, the feet to earth. When a dancer performs a Karana, this cosmic mapping is activated. The dance hall becomes a yantra, the dancer's body a living mandala.

The Rigvedic hymn to Hiranyagarbha describes creation as a primordial vibration that condensed into form through rhythm. The Vedic metres — Gayatri, Trishtubh, Jagati, Anushtubh — are not merely poetic forms; they are rhythmic templates of cosmic manifestation. Bharata directly maps the Karanas to these metres: certain Karanas embody the Gayatri's tri-partite vigour, others the stately sweep of Jagati, others the quick intelligence of Anushtubh. The dance thus becomes a moving recitation of the cosmos's own metrical self-expression.

Presence in Temples and Dance Traditions

Perhaps nowhere is the cosmic significance of the Karanas more visibly proclaimed than upon the stone walls of India's great temples. In the eleventh and twelfth centuries CE, the Chola kings built the magnificent Brihadeeshwara temple at Thanjavur and the Nataraja temple at Chidambaram, commissioning sculptors to carve all 108 Karanas in stone — a permanent, encyclopaedic proclamation that the temple walls themselves were a body of divine dance.

At Chidambaram — the Tillai kshetra, the sacred grove of consciousness — the golden hall of the dancing Shiva (Kanaka Sabha) is surrounded by the carved figures of Karanas. These are not decorative friezes; they are teaching monuments. The inscriptions accompanying several Karanas provide the Sanskrit names from the Natya Shastra alongside the visual form, functioning as a three-dimensional textbook of sacred movement.

In the living traditions of Bharatanatyam — the classical dance form that grew directly from the devadasi temple ritual of Tamil Nadu — the Karanas have been transmitted through guru-shishya parampara (teacher-disciple lineage) across millennia. The Adavus (basic movement units) of Bharatanatyam are directly derived from the Karanas. The Natya Shastra's Karanas are thus not historical curiosities but living seeds from which the entire tree of classical dance continues to grow.

Beyond Bharatanatyam, the Karanas are present — in varying degrees of explicitness — in Odissi, Kuchipudi, Mohiniyattam, and Manipuri. The Karanas represent the common root-language of India's diverse classical performance traditions, each of which is a regional dialect of the same cosmic tongue.

Mudras in Karanas

The Meaning and Essence of Mudra

The Sanskrit word mudra carries extraordinary semantic richness. Etymologically, it derives from the root mud — to delight, to gladden — combined with the causative suffix ra: "that which gives joy" or "that which seals and delights." The Yoga-Tattva Upanishad defines mudra as: mude raatiti mudra — that which bestows (raati) joy (mude). But mudra also means a seal, a sign, a stamp, a gesture that makes manifest an inner reality upon the outer surface of the body.

In the context of the 108 Karanas, every mudra is a word in the sacred body-language. The hands — which the Natya Shastra calls "the instruments through which heaven speaks upon earth" — configure themselves into precise hasta mudras that carry cosmological, devotional, philosophical, and therapeutic meanings. The Abhinaya Darpana of Nandikesvara lists 28 asamyuta hastas (single-hand gestures) and 24 samyuta hastas (combined gestures), each carrying specified meanings (viniyoga) across ritual, narrative, and symbolic registers.

In the Indian philosophical worldview, the human hand is a microcosmic map of the cosmos. Each finger corresponds to one of the five great elements (pancha mahabhuta): the thumb to fire (agni), the index finger to air (vayu), the middle finger to space/ether (akasha), the ring finger to earth (prithvi), the little finger to water (apas). When fingers join in a mudra, they create a specific elemental configuration, modulating the flow of prana within the body.

What Mudras Indicate

Offering — Arpanam

The Arala hasta — the curved, bent finger gesture — is the quintessential gesture of offering. It appears in the Karana called Alata when the raised hand curves gently, mimicking the movement of pouring oblation into sacred fire. The offering encoded in Karana mudras is total: the dancer offers their ego, their breath, their movement, their very being into the fire of consciousness.

Invocation — Avahana

The Abhaya hasta — the open palm raised — appears across multiple Karanas as the mudra of divine invocation and protection. The Pataka mudra (all four fingers extended, thumb bent) is the mudra of invocation par excellence: it initiates, opens sacred space, calls down divine presence. Many Karanas begin with Pataka, establishing the sacred container within which the subsequent movement unfolds.

Protection — Abhaya

The Simhamukha hasta — the lion-face gesture — appears in Karanas associated with fierce protective deities. According to the Agama texts, this mudra generates a protective vibrational field around the performer and the sacred space.

Philosophy — Chinmudra and Jnana Hasta

The Chinmudra — thumb and index finger joined in a circle, remaining three fingers extended — represents the union of individual consciousness (jiva) with universal consciousness (Brahman). The closed circle of thumb and index finger is the circle of wholeness; the three extended fingers represent the three states of waking, dreaming, and deep sleep transcended by the fourth state (turiya) of pure awareness.

Yoga Tattva and the Holistic Human Existence

The Yoga Tattva Upanishad speaks of the human body as a yantra — a sacred instrument — within which five sheaths (pancha kosha) of existence nest: the physical body (annamaya kosha), the vital/breath body (pranamaya kosha), the mental body (manomaya kosha), the wisdom body (vijnanamaya kosha), and the bliss body (anandamaya kosha). The mudras of the Karanas simultaneously address all five koshas.

When a dancer performs a Karana with its prescribed mudra, the physical body is shaped; the breath is channelled; the mind is focused through drishti and bhava; wisdom is activated through the recognition of the mudra's cosmic meaning; and bliss arises spontaneously when all these levels synchronise. This is why the Natya Shastra insists that dance is yoga — indeed, it calls the accomplished dancer a yogi who has achieved samadhi not on a meditation cushion but in full movement.

The repeated performance of the 108 Karanas, taken in their entirety, is a complete kundalini journey: beginning at the earth-rooted Karanas of the first quarter (associated with the muladhara and svadhisthana chakras), ascending through the fiery second quarter (manipura), the heart-opening third quarter (anahata and vishuddha), to the transcendent fourth quarter associated with ajna and sahasrara. The dancer who completes the full cycle of 108 has traversed the entire vertical axis of consciousness.

The Number 108 — Sacred Numerology

Why 108?

The number 108 is among the most sacred numbers in the entire Hindu-Buddhist-Jain cosmological tradition. It is not an arbitrary count but a number that recurs with uncanny consistency across astronomy, physiology, mathematics, and theology — each recurrence pointing toward the same fundamental truth about the structure of reality.

In Vedic astronomy, the distance between the Earth and the Sun is approximately 108 times the Sun's diameter. The distance between the Earth and the Moon is approximately 108 times the Moon's diameter. This extraordinary astronomical fact was known to ancient Indian astronomers and interpreted as cosmic confirmation that 108 is literally built into the fabric of the solar-terrestrial relationship.

The human body itself vibrates with 108. There are 108 marma points — sacred energy intersections — mapped in Ayurvedic medicine, each a junction of prana, blood, and nervous tissue. There are 108 nadis (subtle energy channels) that converge upon the anahata (heart) chakra. The Sanskrit alphabet contains 54 letters, each appearing in both masculine (Shiva) and feminine (Shakti) aspects, yielding 108 phonemic energies.

Division into 4 × 27: The Cosmic Quartet

Bharata divides the 108 Karanas into four groups of 27, corresponding to the four Vedas, the four cardinal directions, the four seasons, and the four aims of human existence (purusharthas): Dharma, Artha, Kama, and Moksha.

The number 27 carries its own profound significance. The moon's sidereal cycle takes 27.3 days to complete one revolution around the Earth. Vedic astronomy divides the zodiac into 27 lunar mansions (nakshatras), each ruled by a specific divine force. The 27 Karanas of each quarter are therefore not random groupings but precise cosmic correspondences with the 27 nakshatras, mapping the dance vocabulary onto the architecture of the heavens.

Philosophical Meaning of 108

The number 108 is 1 + 0 + 8 = 9 — the number of completion and cosmic wholeness in Vedic numerology. Most profoundly, the Upanishads teach that 108 represents the totality of human spiritual aspiration: 1 stands for the Supreme Self (Brahman), 0 for the emptiness of ego (shunyata), and 8 for the eightfold path (ashtanga) of yoga, ethics, and consciousness. Together they constitute the complete map of the journey from individual self to cosmic Self.

The philosopher Abhinavagupta wrote that beauty (saundarya) is nothing other than the recognition (pratyabhijna) of one's own innermost nature reflected in the outer form. The 108 Karanas, embodying 108 modalities of cosmic beauty, are thus 108 opportunities for this recognition — each Karana a mirror in which the dancer may recognise themselves as the very dance of consciousness they are enacting.

Temple Sculptures — Stone as Cosmic Scripture

Why Karanas Were Carved in Stone

The decision to carve the 108 Karanas upon temple walls was, at its deepest level, a theological act. The Agama Shastra teaches that a properly constructed temple is not a building in which God is housed but a materialised body of God. The shikhara (spire) is the head, the garbhagriha (inner sanctum) is the womb, the corridors are the arteries, and the outer walls are the skin. When Karanas are carved upon the skin of the temple, the temple itself is shown to be a dancing body.

There is also a yogic logic to the placement. The devotee who circumambulates the temple (pradakshina) encounters the Karanas in sequence as they move clockwise around the sanctum. The sequence of Karanas encountered during pradakshina is not random but follows the cosmic order of the four quarters: the devotee physically enacts a meditative journey through the 108 stages of consciousness simply by walking around the temple.

Chidambaram — The Tillai Cosmic Stage

The Nataraja temple at Chidambaram in Tamil Nadu is the supreme example of Karana sculpture. In the eastern gopuram (gateway tower), all 108 Karanas are depicted with exceptional care: each figure is accompanied by inscriptions in Tamil and Sanskrit giving the Karana's name and technical description, creating a living Natya Shastra in stone. The Chidambaram Karanas were carved during the Chola period (9th–12th century CE).

The Nataraja enshrined at Chidambaram is the Akasha Nataraja — Shiva as the cosmic dancer in space, the element of pure consciousness. The Chidambara Rahasya (Secret of Chidambaram) is that the innermost sanctum, behind a golden curtain, contains not an idol but a void — the akasha itself. The surrounding Karanas, then, are the movements of this void, the dance that arises from and returns to the silence of pure being.

Thanjavur Brihadeeshwara — The Great Chola Vision

Built by Raja Raja Chola I between 1003 and 1010 CE, the Brihadeeshwara temple at Thanjavur rises 66 metres and was conceived as a three-dimensional yantra encoding cosmological principles. The Karana sculptures appear in the circumambulatory corridor and upon the outer walls of the ardhamandapa (antechamber), integrated into a larger programme including devadasi dancers and celestial musicians. The Thanjavur carvings emphasise the continuity between divine dance and the human devadasis serving the temple — who performed the Karanas daily as part of the temple ritual.

Other Sacred Sites

The Airavatesvara temple at Darasuram contains exquisite Karana sculptures integrated with a broader programme of Shaiva mythology, placing the Karanas in dialogue with the 64 divine arts (chausath kala). In Andhra Pradesh, the Lepakshi temple (Vijayanagara period, 16th century) features extraordinary ceiling paintings of dancing figures in Karana postures. The Konark Sun temple's natya mandapa (dance hall) in Odisha carries sculptural representations of classical dance that share the same metaphysical premise: the temple wall is the cosmic dancer's body, and the carvings are its eternal performance.

Names & Divisions of the 108 Karanas

The Four Quarters — A Cosmic Taxonomy

The 108 Karanas are divided into four groups (chatushpadas) of 27 each. These four groups correspond to the four Vedas, four great elements, four life-aims, four traditional stages of life, and four directions. Together, they constitute a complete map of existence — from the most densely material to the most sublimely transcendent.

First Quarter — Karanas 1–27: The Earth Dimension

The first 27 Karanas are rooted in the earth element (prithvi), grounded and foundational. Their movement quality is weighted, deliberate, and steady. They correspond to the Rig Veda — the hymns of praise that anchor cosmic truth in specific, name-able forms.

1Sthānaka
Standing at ease; the body as the world-pillar (skambha). Grounded awareness, the still centre of the spinning world.
2Āyata
The wide-open stance; cosmic expansion into the four directions. The first movement of creation — a divine outbreathing.
3Alīḍha
The archer's stance, right foot forward; the directed will aiming at the heart of dharma. Corresponds to Indra drawing the celestial bow.
4Pratyālīḍha
Mirror stance to Alīḍha; the left-handed archer. The balance of solar and lunar principles, action and reflection.
5Swastika
Cross-legged standing; the svastika (well-being) mandala made flesh. The four arms of cosmic order meeting at the axis of the human spine.
6Motita
Shoulders raised, arms encircling; the gesture of encompassing all beings in compassion. The cosmic mother-posture.
7Sama
Absolute equilibrium; all forces in perfect balance. The state before the first movement — pure potential.
8Vaiṣhṇava
The standing posture of Vishnu; right hand raised in benediction. The sustaining principle of the cosmos made visible in the body.
9Maṇḍala
The circular posture; the body as mandala. The cosmic circle whose centre is everywhere and circumference nowhere.

Second Quarter — Karanas 28–54: Fire and Water

The second 27 Karanas belong to the realm of fire and water — the dynamic, transformative elements. Their quality is energetic, flowing, and powerful. They correspond to the Yajur Veda — the Veda of sacred action, sacrifice, and transformation.

28Nikuṭṭaka
Bent knees, hands at chest level; the crouching flame. The fire that burns beneath the surface of ordinary consciousness.
29Udvritta
Spiralling upward; the column of fire rising from earth to heaven. Corresponds to the agnistoma fire-pillar of Vedic ritual.
36Vivarta
Complete turn; full revolution. The wheel of karma completing its circuit, dharma reasserting itself after every deviation.
45Nūpura
Ankle-bell movement; the sound of the cosmos stepping. The nūpura (anklet) announces the divine arrival.
54Gajakrīḍita
Elephant's play; majestic, unhurried, irresistible. The movement of gajendra (noble elephant) who knows his own weight and uses it wisely.

Third Quarter — Karanas 55–81: The Aerial Dance

The third group of 27 Karanas embodies the air and space principles — expansive, lyrical, and expressive. They correspond to the Sama Veda — the Veda of celestial song — and their movement quality is that of music made visible.

55Bhramaraka
Spinning like a bee; the joyful circumambulation of the beloved. Echoes the madhukari (honeybee's) devotional wandering from flower to flower.
63Kṣipta
Thrown into space; the ecstatic release of the arrow of prayer. The prana liberated from bodily confines.
72Urūdvritta
Thigh-circling; the cosmic spiral encoded in the motion of the hip. The serpent kundalini beginning its ascent.
81Tala Saṃsphoṭita
Palm-clap into space; the sound that creates. In the beginning was the Word — and this Karana enacts that primordial utterance.

Fourth Quarter — Karanas 82–108: The Transcendent

The final 27 Karanas represent the ether element (akasha) and correspond to the Atharva Veda. Their movement quality is paradoxical: simultaneously the most complex in external form and the most simple in inner quality. They move toward stillness, toward the great silence that underlies all sound.

82Nitamba
The hip-sway of divine beauty; the movement of Shakti in her most intimate self-revelation. Maya (divine creative power) dancing for her own delight.
99Uromaṇḍala
Chest-circle; the heart-chakra opened to receive the universe. The anahata opening in its fullness.
108Ūrdhva-Jaṃgha
Raised shin; the final gesture of transcendence. One foot upon the earth, one shin reaching toward heaven. The ananda tāṇḍava of Nataraja — bliss dancing upon ignorance. The circle closes; the dance is complete — and immediately begins again.

Connections — The Karanas in Cosmic Dialogue

I. The Vedic Connection — Agni, Indra, and Soma

The Rig Veda opens with a hymn to Agni — the divine fire, the first priest, the carrier of offerings between the human and divine realms. The Natya Shastra's very first Karana is a posture of establishment and steadiness, corresponding to the Agni of the sacrificial altar who must be lit before any ritual can proceed. Bharata explicitly states that the Natya Veda is a sacrifice (yajna) in which the dancer's body is the altar, the movement is the oblation, and the audience's rasa-experience is the divine acceptance of the offering.

Indra — the king of the gods, the wielder of the thunderbolt (vajra), the champion of order against chaos — presides over the dynamic, powerful Karanas of the second quarter. The Indra-related Karanas are those involving strong foot-stamping (tattit), wide power-stances (pratyalidha), and explosive extensions of the arms (kshipta). These are the Karanas that Indra himself might have performed when he slew Vritra, the cosmic serpent of drought and stagnation, with his lightning-bolt dance of liberation.

Soma — the mysterious moon-plant whose pressed juice was the ritual drink of the Vedic gods — governs the fluid, lyrical Karanas of the third quarter. The Soma-Karanas flow and spiral like the pressed juice, like moonlight on water, like the meditative awareness that flows effortlessly from object to object without grasping or resistance. The Shringara (love) and Shanta (peace) rasas correspond to the sweetness and luminous calm of Soma's influence upon consciousness.

The three Vedic fires — Garhapatya (the household fire), Ahavaniya (the offering fire), and Dakshina (the southern fire) — have their structural analogue in the three qualities of movement (gunas) that pervade the Karanas: tamas (inertia), rajas (dynamic activity), and sattva (luminous clarity). Every Karana can be mapped onto this tripartite guna-fire schema, and the full cycle of 108 represents the progressive purification of tamas through rajas into sattvic luminosity.

II. Cosmological Connection — The Five Elements

The five great elements (pancha mahabhuta) — Earth, Water, Fire, Air, and Ether — are not merely physical substances but cosmic principles that organise reality at every level. The 108 Karanas encode all five elements in their internal structure.

Earth (Prithvi) enters the Karanas through the feet. The Taṇḍava (masculine, vigorous dance) tradition emphasises earth energy through its dramatic foot-work. The foot's contact with the earth is not merely physical but energetic: each foot-stamp in a Karana activates the muladhara chakra's external expression, grounding the dancer's energy and awakening primal vitality.

Water (Apas) manifests in the Karanas through the quality of continuous flow — the Lasya (feminine, flowing dance) tradition's characteristic wave-like movement. The water principle is present in every circular or spiral movement: the rolling of the wrists, the flowing rotation of the torso, the wave-like progression of spinal movement from the base upward.

Fire (Agni) blazes in the leaping Karanas — the utplutas (jumps) that appear particularly in the second quarter. Fire-Karanas are characterised by sudden uplift, by the explosive release of accumulated potential. The feet leave the earth, the body becomes temporarily weightless, and in that suspended moment of flight, the dancer enacts the condition of pure fire — liberated from gravity, burning upward toward its own source.

Air (Vayu) is the principle of the third quarter: the flowing, swirling movements associated with the Samana and Udana pranas. The air-Karanas are characterised by effortless extension — the arms opening like wings, the spine unfurling like a wind-moved flag, the gaze travelling far into the distance as the air-element naturally seeks the horizon.

Ether (Akasha) pervades the final quarter and is simultaneously present in every Karana as the awareness within which all movement occurs. The Chidambaram Nataraja, as the Akasha Nataraja, teaches that the deepest teaching of the Karanas is the recognition of the ether — the silent, pervading consciousness — that makes all movement possible.

III. Musical Connection — Sama Veda, Rhythm, and Melody

The Sama Veda is the most musical of the four Vedas — its hymns, drawn largely from the Rig Veda, are set to elaborate melodic patterns and sung by the Udgatar priests during the Soma sacrifice. The Sama Veda represents the moment when cosmic vibration finds its most refined expression: the moment when truth (sat) becomes beauty (saundarya) through the medium of organised sound.

Bharata's Natya Shastra establishes the principle of samgraha (confluence): the highest aesthetic experience arises only when nritta (pure movement), natya (dramatic expression), and sangita (music) are perfectly unified. The Karanas are fundamentally musical: each Karana has a prescribed tala (rhythmic cycle) and a prescribed emotional quality (rasa) that is supported by the musical raga (melodic framework) being performed.

The 72 Melakarta ragas — the parent ragas of Carnatic classical music — provide the melodic universe within which the Karanas are performed. The Karanas of the first quarter naturally correspond to ragas of grounded, devotional character. The fire-Karanas of the second quarter align with ragas of heroic, dynamic character such as Hamsadhwani and Desh. The lyrical third-quarter Karanas resonate with the fluid beauty of Yaman, Bhairavi, and Kapi. The transcendent fourth-quarter Karanas correspond to ragas associated with late night and dawn — Kalyani and Todi.

The 22 shrutis (microtonal intervals) of the Indian octave are not merely divisions of a musical scale but qualitative energies — each carrying a specific emotional and pranic quality. The seven svaras — Sa, Ri, Ga, Ma, Pa, Da, Ni — are not merely musical notes but cosmic vibrational principles. Sa corresponds to the earth element and the muladhara; Ri to water and svadhisthana; Ga to fire and manipura; Ma to air and anahata; Pa to ether and vishuddha; Da to the ajna chakra; Ni to the boundary of the sahasrara. Each of the 108 Karanas activates a specific point along this svara-chakra continuum.

Strong Da, strong Dha, strong Ni — the upper register svaras — correspond to the transcendent Karanas of the fourth quarter. When performed with full understanding, each Karana is simultaneously a physical posture, a raga-phrase, and a chakra-activation: body, sound, and consciousness unified in a single, indivisible act of sacred art.

IV. Tantric Connection — Sri Chakra and Shakti

Tantra is the tradition of sacred technology — practices that use the body, sound, image, and ritual action as instruments for the direct experience of divine reality. The Karana tradition, viewed through the Tantric lens, reveals itself as one of the most complete and sophisticated Tantric practices available: a complete sadhana that engages every dimension of the human being simultaneously.

The Sri Yantra (Sri Chakra) is the primary geometric symbol of the Shakta Tantric tradition — the visual representation of the divine feminine reality (Shakti) in her fullest cosmic expression. It consists of nine interlocking triangles — four pointing upward (Shiva, consciousness) and five pointing downward (Shakti, power) — creating 43 smaller triangles (avaranas or enclosures), each presided over by a specific aspect of the divine feminine.

The 108 Karanas map onto the Sri Chakra in a profoundly structured way. The four quarters of 27 Karanas correspond to the four outermost layers of the Sri Chakra. The innermost layer — the Bindu, the point of pure consciousness — corresponds to the state of sthiti (stillness) that is the ground of all 108 movements. Just as the Sri Chakra's geometry encodes the dynamic play of Shiva and Shakti in every possible configuration, the 108 Karanas encode the full range of the divine dance in every possible combination of masculine (Taṇḍava) and feminine (Lasya) principles.

Lalitha Tripura Sundari — the supreme deity of the Sri Vidya tradition — is described in the Lalitha Sahasranama with names that directly correspond to specific Karanas: Nritya Priya (lover of dance), Natanaramba (initiator of dance), Lasya Priya (lover of the Lasya style). Both the Sahasranama's 1000 names and the 108 Karanas are complete expressions of the same ultimate reality — one from the perspective of sound, one from the perspective of movement.

V. Philosophical Connection — Unity, Duality, Cycles, Transcendence

The Advaita Vedanta teaching of non-duality finds its most perfect expression in the Karana tradition's insistence on the unity of all four elements of dance: the hasta (hands), the pada (feet), the sharira (body), and the drishthi (gaze) must become one. When they become one — when the dancer loses the sense of being a person performing a dance and simply becomes the dance itself — this is not merely aesthetic achievement but Advaitic realisation.

Duality is the condition from which the Karanas begin. The body is dual: right and left, front and back, above and below. The dance begins in this duality — the Alīḍha and Pratyālīḍha are literally mirror-image Karanas, enacting the cosmic polarity of solar and lunar, masculine and feminine. The Karanas do not negate duality; they dance through it, celebrating its richness while demonstrating its ultimate non-separation.

The cyclical nature of existence is encoded in the circular structure of the 108 Karanas. The cycle begins with Sthānaka (standing) and ends with Ūrdhva-Jaṃgha (raised shin) — and the end immediately opens back to the beginning. Like the year that ends at the winter solstice and immediately begins again, the Karana-cycle demonstrates the sacred truth that every ending is a beginning and every moment of stillness is pregnant with the next movement.

Transcendence is the ultimate destination of the Karana journey, but the Karanas insist that transcendence does not mean the abandonment of the world but its full embrace and illumination. The final Karanas do not still the body; they liberate movement from the gravity of ego. This is the Tantric teaching of jivanmukti: liberation while living, enlightenment while embodied. The 108 Karanas are a complete curriculum for this most demanding and most generous form of liberation.

Prakriti & Purusha — Material Nature and Consciousness United in Dance

The Primordial Dualism

The Samkhya philosophical system posits a primordial dualism at the heart of existence: Prakriti and Purusha. Prakriti is primordial matter: the undifferentiated, dynamic, creative feminine principle that contains within itself the seed of all manifest reality. Purusha is primordial consciousness: the unmoving, witnessing, masculine principle of pure awareness that illuminates Prakriti without being touched by her transformations.

In the Samkhya cosmology, the universe comes into existence when Purusha and Prakriti come into proximity. The stillness of Purusha's witnessing gaze falls upon the dynamic field of Prakriti, and Prakriti — aware of being seen — begins her creation-dance. She differentiates from primordial undifferentiation into the hierarchy of manifest existence: cosmic intellect (mahat), individual selfhood (ahamkara), subtle elements (tanmatras), and finally the gross elements (mahabhuta). The entire creation is Prakriti's dance before the witness of Purusha.

The 108 Karanas are the most precise possible encoding of this dynamic. The outer form of each Karana — the posture, the gesture, the movement — is Prakriti in her most refined artistic expression. The inner witnessing awareness that the dancer maintains even within the most dynamic movements — the sakshibhava (witness-stance) — is Purusha. To perform a Karana with full understanding is to simultaneously be both: the dancer who moves (Prakriti) and the consciousness that witnesses the dancing (Purusha), united in the indivisible wholeness of the dance moment.

The Taṇḍava and Lasya as Cosmic Principles

The classical tradition distinguishes two fundamental modes of dance: Taṇḍava, the vigorous, dynamic style associated with Shiva (Purusha), and Lasya, the flowing, graceful style associated with Parvati/Shakti (Prakriti). The 108 Karanas include both forms. When a male dancer performs Lasya Karanas, or a female dancer performs Taṇḍava Karanas, the tradition makes a profound philosophical statement: that each human being contains both Purusha and Prakriti, and the complete dancer is one who has integrated both principles in their body-knowledge.

The Ardhanarisvara — Shiva as half-male and half-female, divided vertically — is the most explicit visual statement of this integration. The Ardhanarisvara Karana is among the most challenging and most sacred of the 108: it requires the dancer to simultaneously inhabit both qualities, demonstrating in their living body the indivisibility of consciousness and matter, silence and sound, witness and dance.

The Body as Sacred Intersection

The Chandogya Upanishad teaches tat tvam asi — "That thou art" — declaring the identity of the individual self (atman) with the universal self (Brahman). The 108 Karanas are the embodied practice of this identity. The human body, in the Tantric understanding, is the supreme yantra — the sacred geometric diagram that maps the entire cosmos. The sushumna nadi (central energy channel) running from the base of the spine to the crown of the head is the vertical axis of the cosmos (Mount Meru, the world-pillar). When the Karanas are performed with knowledge of this inner architecture, each movement becomes a conscious act of cosmic realignment.

Part Eight · 4 Pages

Cosmic Grammar — 108 Karanas as Syllables, Hymns, and Scripture

The Body-Language of the Cosmos

In the Vedic tradition, Vak (Speech) is not merely a human capacity but a divine reality. The Rig Veda's famous Vak Sukta declares that Vak pervades all of existence: she is in the gods, in the animals, in the stones, in the rivers, in the stars. Language is not something humans invented; it is something cosmic reality speaks, and humans partially hear. Within this framework, the 108 Karanas are a form of Vak — specifically, kaya-vak (body-speech), the speech of the body-cosmos.

Just as the Vedic Sanskrit language has its aksharas (syllables), its words (pada), its sentences (vakya), and its complete hymns (sukta), the Karana language has its elemental gestures (mudra and hasta), its complete Karanas (the body-words), its sequential narrative combinations (varṇa), and its complete expressions of cosmic themes (the dance-hymns performed in temple ritual). Panini's Ashtadhyayi performs an exactly analogous operation to the Natya Shastra: just as Panini generates all grammatical forms from root-forms (dhatu), Bharata generates the entire vocabulary of Indian classical dance from 108 Karana root-forms.

Karanas as Cosmic Syllables

The Sanskrit tradition recognises three levels of language: Vaikhari (audible sound), Madhyama (subtle mental language), and Para (transcendental, unmanifest word-ground). The 108 Karanas correspond to these three levels simultaneously. At the Vaikhari level, the Karana is the visible physical movement. At the Madhyama level, the Karana is the bhava (inner emotional state) that the dancer carries within the movement. At the Para level, the Karana is the spanda — the cosmic vibration — that underlies both the movement and the feeling.

The 51 letters of the Sanskrit alphabet are called matrika — "little mothers" — in the Tantric tradition. The Tantric body map places each of the 51 matrikas at a specific location in the body. When a dancer performs a Karana, they activate specific matrika-locations, literally pronouncing the cosmic alphabet through bodily movement. The 108 Karanas, containing various combinations of these body-syllable activations, are thus hymns composed in the language of the body's own cosmic alphabet.

Dance as Living Scripture

In the Vedic tradition, shruti (that which is heard — the Vedas) is distinguished from smriti (that which is remembered — the Puranas) as the more authoritative category of sacred literature. But the Karanas constitute a third category: drishtya — that which is seen — and simultaneously sparshya — that which is felt through the body. They constitute the knowledge of the living body enacting divine truth.

The Agama texts describe three types of puja (worship): manasika (mental), vachika (verbal), and kayika (bodily). The 108 Karanas constitute the supreme form of kayika puja: the offering of the entire body — every joint, every muscle, every breath, every neural pathway — as a living altar upon which the sacred fire of consciousness is maintained. When a devadasi performed the Karanas before the temple deity, her entire embodied being had become the prayer. The Vedic concept of Rta — the cosmic order — is made visible in the Karanas: when a Karana is performed correctly, it embodies Rta directly. It is not a representation of cosmic order but an instance of it.

Part Nine · 5 Pages

The Science of Sound — Nada, Resonance, and Embodiment

Nada Brahma — The Universe as Sound

The Vedic tradition proclaims Nada Brahma: the universe is sound. This is not a metaphor. The Rig Veda opens with the syllable A; the Upanishads teach that AUM (OM) is the pranava — the fundamental vibration from which all other vibrations derive. Modern physics, through string theory, proposes that the fundamental constituents of matter are not particles but vibrating one-dimensional strings. The ancient Vedic teaching and the modern scientific hypothesis converge upon the same fundamental insight.

The Nada Bindu Upanishad teaches that sound (nada) has two fundamental forms: ahata (struck sound, audible to the ears) and anahata (unstruck sound, the internal vibration that underlies all audible sound). The anahata nada is the cosmic sound-ground that underlies all manifestation — the silence that is not the absence of sound but the presence of all possible sounds simultaneously. The Karanas are simultaneously performances in ahata sound — the ankle-bells, the foot-rhythms, the music — and expressions of anahata sound — the dancer's own experience of the subtle vibrational reality of each gesture.

Dhwani Theory — Resonance Beyond the Word

The Dhwani (resonance) theory is the pinnacle achievement of classical Sanskrit literary aesthetics. Developed by Anandavardhana in his Dhvanyaloka (9th century CE) and extended by Abhinavagupta in his Locana, the theory proposes that the highest function of aesthetic language is not the direct statement of meaning (vacyartha) but the suggestion of a resonant, reverberating deeper meaning (dhvani) that arises in the sensitive audience member like the sound that continues after the struck note has ceased.

The word dhwani means literally the sound that resonates — the after-tone, the overtone, the harmonic generated by the fundamental note but not identical with it. A bell struck once produces not only its fundamental frequency but a complex spectrum of overtones that together create the bell's distinctive voice. A great dance works exactly this way: the stated content is the fundamental note, but the resonant meaning — the aesthetic experience — is the dhwani, the overtone that arises in the space between the art and the receptive consciousness.

Abhinavagupta makes the extraordinary claim that rasa-experience is identical with brahmanubhava (the experience of Brahman). When a supremely cultivated audience member (sahridaya — one with a prepared heart) witnesses a perfectly performed Karana and is flooded with aesthetic bliss, they are not merely enjoying art; they are directly experiencing the nature of reality. The dhwani of the Karana reverberates through all the layers of the perceiver's consciousness until it reaches the bedrock of pure awareness — and in that moment, the distinction between art and meditation, between aesthetic experience and spiritual realisation, dissolves completely.

Svara, Shruti, and the Embodied Scale

The 22 shrutis of the Indian musical octave represent the most precise mapping of the tonal universe ever achieved by any musical culture. The seven svaras — Sa, Ri, Ga, Ma, Pa, Da, Ni — are distributed across these 22 shrutis in specific ways depending on the raga. The tonic Sa always occupies the first shruti; the natural Pa (perfect fifth) always occupies the 13th shruti.

In performance, the dancer attuned to the musical accompaniment experiences the shrutis not merely as pitches but as somatic sensations. The low Sa creates a sensation of rootedness at the base of the spine; the Ri and Ga bring energy to the lower abdomen and solar plexus; the Ma and Pa resonate in the chest and heart; the upper Da and Ni carry energy to the throat and upper face; the higher octave Sa vibrates at the crown. The dancer who is simultaneously responsive to the shruti-energies and performing the Karanas is performing a continuous somatic meditation.

The strong forms of the upper svaras — the tivra (sharp) versions of Ri, Ga, Da, and Ni — carry particular intensity and are associated with the most exalted states of consciousness. In Karana performance, the moments when these intense shrutis are sounded in the accompaniment are precisely the moments for the most technically demanding and spiritually significant Karanas: the leaps, the extreme extensions, the sustained balances on one foot that demand both perfect technique and perfect surrender. The music, in these moments, literally lifts the dancer — not metaphorically but energetically, through the resonance of shruti upon shruti as the body's own subtle sound-field harmonises with the music's dominant frequencies.

The vowels of the Sanskrit language correspond directly to the 12 semitones of the Indian octave. When a dancer performs a Karana and the accompanying singer holds a prolonged vowel — the 'aa' of deep resonance, the 'i' of crystalline clarity, the 'u' of rounded depth — the vowel-sound enters the dancer's body through the ear and activates the corresponding somatic centre. This is why the Natya Shastra insists that the dancer must also be a musician: the understanding of nada from within — the direct experience of sound as embodied reality — is inseparable from the fullest expression of the Karanas.

Part Ten · 2 Pages

Summation — Dance as Cosmic Dialogue

The Integration of All Themes

We have traversed an immense territory: from the precise technical definitions of the Natya Shastra to the metaphysical heights of Kashmir Shaivism; from the temple walls of Chidambaram to the innermost geometry of the Sri Chakra; from the 22 shrutis of Carnatic music to the 108 matrika-syllables of the cosmic body-alphabet. And throughout this vast traversal, one truth has shown itself with increasing clarity and force: the 108 Karanas are not a collection of dance techniques but a complete philosophy of existence expressed in the most immediate and undeniable medium available to the human being — the living, breathing, moving body.

The Karanas integrate, in a single comprehensive system, all the major streams of the Indian intellectual and spiritual tradition: the ritual precision of the Vedic yajna; the metaphysical inquiry of the Upanishads; the cosmological mapping of the Samkhya and Yoga systems; the devotional intensity of the Agama and Tantra traditions; the aesthetic theory of the Natyashastra and the Dhvanyaloka; the musical science of the 72 Melakarta ragas and the 22 shrutis; the philosophical sophistication of Advaita, Shaiva Siddhanta, and Kashmir Shaivism. These streams converge within the space of the Karana like rivers flowing into the same ocean — the ocean of embodied consciousness.

Dance as Cosmic Dialogue

Dialogue requires two parties. In ordinary human dialogue, two separate individuals exchange words. In the cosmic dialogue of the Karanas, the parties are the dancer's individual consciousness and the universal consciousness of the cosmos — and the extraordinary revelation of the tradition is that these two parties are not truly separate. The dancer speaks; the cosmos responds through the dancer's own body. The cosmos speaks through the music, the rhythm, the audience's attentive presence; the dancer responds through the Karanas.

This is the ultimate gift of the Karana tradition: the transformation of ordinary embodied existence into an act of continuous cosmic creativity and devotion. The 108 Karanas teach us that the body is not a prison from which consciousness must escape but a temple in which consciousness can be most beautifully expressed. They teach us that movement is not a distraction from the spiritual life but one of its supreme expressions. They teach us that the universe loves us enough to speak to us in the language we know best — the language of the body, the language of gesture and rhythm and breath — and that when we learn to hear this language and speak it back, we discover that we have always been, and will always be, dancing in the arms of the eternal.

Nartana sharire brahma nishkalam — In the dancing body, Brahman shines without division.

Karana Foundations

Reference 1: Natya Shastra / Abhinaya Darpana

The Natya Shastra of Bharata Muni (200 BCE–200 CE) defines all 108 Karanas in its fourth chapter with the formula: name, foot-position, hand-configuration, body-posture, and movement-quality. It declares these Karanas to be the "building blocks of all movement in the universe."

NS 4.29–32
Bharata defines Karana: "The simultaneous movement of hasta and pada, when combined with appropriate sharira, is called Karana by the learned. These Karanas are the root of all movement in natya, nritta, and nritya."

The Abhinaya Darpana of Nandikesvara (approximately 10th century CE) supplements the Natya Shastra with a complete enumeration of single-hand gestures (asamyuta hasta), combined gestures (samyuta hasta), and their specific meanings (viniyoga).

Reference 2: Rig Veda & Yajur Veda

The Rig Veda establishes the sacred ontology within which the Karanas operate. The Purusha Sukta (RV 10.90) describes the cosmic body whose dismemberment and reassembly creates the universe — the prototype of the dancer's body as cosmic instrument.

RV 10.90.1
Purusha Sukta: "Thousand-headed Purusha, thousand-eyed, thousand-footed — encompassing the earth on all sides, he extends ten fingers' breadths beyond it." The thousand-fold body is enacted through the 108 Karanas.

The Yajur Veda establishes the ritual science of precise bodily movement as sacred act. The adhvaryu priest's choreography of the Soma sacrifice — with exactly prescribed movements, orientations, and gestures — is the direct ritual predecessor of the Karana tradition.

Reference 1: Ashta Vasus — Elemental Guardians of Cosmic Order

The eight Vasus (Ashta Vasus) — Dhruva, Dhara, Anala, Aha, Anila, Apas, Prabhasa, and Pratyusha — embody the eight fundamental aspects of cosmic order and elemental reality. Each Vasu governs one aspect of the created world and corresponds to one quality of Karana movement. The eight Vasus map onto the eight primary body-joints (ashtanga) that Bharata identifies as the principal sites of expressive movement: neck, chest, sides, waist, hips, thighs, knees, and feet.

Reference 2: Sapta Rishis — Celestial Wisdom Lineage

The seven great sages (Sapta Rishis) are the cosmic antennae through which the primordial knowledge descends into the human realm. In the night sky, they are identified with the seven stars of Ursa Major. The seven Sapta Rishis correspond to the seven classical musical notes (svaras): Sa (Atri), Ri (Bhrigu), Ga (Kuthsa), Ma (Vasishtha), Pa (Gautama), Da (Kashyapa), and Ni (Angiras). The seven svaras provide the melodic framework within which the 108 Karanas are performed.

Mantric Layer

Reference 1: Rudram Namakam & Chamakam

The Sri Rudram (Taittriya Samhita, Krishna Yajur Veda) consists of the Namakam (salutations to Rudra in all his forms) and the Chamakam (requesting every possible blessing). The Namakam's enumeration of Rudra's manifestations is simultaneously a cosmological map and a movement map. The dancer who knows the Sri Rudram recognises in each Karana an embodied salutation to the Rudra-aspect that that Karana expresses.

Reference 2: Lalitha Sahasranamavali

The Lalitha Sahasranama (Brahmanda Purana) describes every aspect of the divine feminine reality. The 108 Karanas find their verbal equivalents within the thousand names. Name 167 — Nritya Priya (lover of dance) — corresponds to the Karana tradition as a whole. Name 168 — Nrita Rupini (whose form is dance) — corresponds to the nature of Nataraja. The complete Sahasranama, chanted while the dancer performs the 108 Karanas, creates a convergence of verbal and somatic meaning that constitutes the most complete possible form of Sri Vidya upasana.

Musical Framework

Reference 1: 22 Shrutis — Microtonal Divisions of Sound

The Natya Shastra (Chapter 28, the Gitadhyaya) first systematically discusses the 22 shrutis, listing them by name and assigning them specific qualities: virya (power), karuna (compassion), hasya (joy), and others. These qualities directly correspond to the emotional qualities (rasas) that the corresponding Karanas are designed to express. The 22 shrutis are distributed across the seven svaras: Sa (4), Ri (3), Ga (2), Ma (4), Pa (4), Da (3), Ni (2) — totalling 22.

Reference 2: 72 Melakarta Ragas — The Melodic Universe

The 72 Melakarta ragas, systematised by Venkatamakhi in his Chaturdandi Prakashika (1660 CE), are the complete system of parent scales used in Carnatic classical music. The number 72 is cosmologically significant: it is the number of years it takes the Earth's precession to shift the vernal equinox by one degree (72 × 360 = 25,920 years for the full cycle). The 72 Melas thus encode both the microcosmic (musical) and macrocosmic (astronomical) cycles simultaneously.

Reference 3: Sa, Ri, Ga, Ma — Svara as Vibrational Gateway

Sa (shadja) — the tonic — means 'born of six': generated by the confluence of the six primary body-resonators (nose, throat, chest, larynx, tongue, teeth). Its vibration is the ground, the root, the cosmic OM at the fundament of all melody. The strong forms of Da, Dha, and Ni in the upper register — combined with the high Sa — correspond to the ascending arc from the heart chakra to the crown, mirroring the transcendent fourth quarter of Karanas where the dancer's movement becomes most subtle, most elevated, and most interior.

Tantric Dimension — Complete Integration

Reference 1: Devi Khadgamala Stotram — Sri Chakra Avaranas

The Devi Khadgamala Stotram (Vamakeshvara Tantra) invokes the divine feminine Shakti in all her manifestations as organised within the Sri Chakra's nine enclosures (avaranas). The hymn proceeds from the outermost square (the bhupura) through progressively subtler enclosures until reaching the innermost Bindu — the dimensionless point of pure consciousness.

The nine avaranas map precisely onto the nine qualitative zones of the 108 Karanas: the outermost bhupura (presided over by the ten Mudra Shaktis) corresponds to the most physically grounded Karanas of the first quarter (Karanas 1–10); the second avaran (the sixteen-petalled lotus, presided over by the sixteen Nitya Shaktis) corresponds to the next sixteen Karanas (11–26). The progression continues inward, with each avaran's divine force corresponding to a specific cluster of Karanas whose movement-quality embodies that divine force's characteristic energy.

The sixteen Nitya Shaktis — Kamesvari, Bhagamalini, Nityaklinna, Bherunda, Vahnivasini, Mahavajresvari, Shivaduti, Tvarita, Kulasundari, Nitya, Nilapataka, Vijaya, Sarvamangala, Jvalamalini, Chitrini, and Mahanitya — are the sixteen phases of the lunar cycle and the sixteen vowels (svaras) of the Sanskrit alphabet. Each Nitya corresponds to a lunar day (tithi), a vowel-sound, a colour, and — through our expanded synthesis — a specific cluster of Karanas. To perform the sixteen Karanas associated with the sixteen Nityas is to enact the complete lunar cycle in bodily movement.

The Devi Khadgamala's final section invokes the divine Khadga (sword of wisdom) that cuts through ignorance — corresponding directly to the quality of the transcendent fourth-quarter Karanas: movements that are simultaneously forceful and subtle, that penetrate through the surface of ordinary consciousness to reveal the luminous awareness beneath. To perform the final Karanas of the 108 with this understanding is to wield the Khadga of Devi with one's own body.

Reference 2: Upanishadic Metaphors — Light, Sound, and Consciousness as Dance

The Mandukya Upanishad's AUM-analysis — dividing the primordial syllable into its four components (A, U, M, and the silence that follows) — corresponds to the four quarters of the 108 Karanas: A to the first quarter (waking, earth, Dharma), U to the second quarter (dreaming, fire, Artha), M to the third quarter (deep sleep, air-water, Kama), and the silence to the fourth quarter (turiya — the fourth state — ether, Moksha).

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad's teaching on the nature of Atman — the self that is found not by searching but by ceasing all activity that obscures it — paradoxically informs the most dynamic Karanas: the very extremity of vigorous movement, when pushed to its limit, tips over into the still point of effortless awareness. The dancer does not find the Atman by stopping; they find it by moving so completely that the mover disappears.

The Kena Upanishad opens with the question: "By what is the eye able to see, the ear able to hear, the mind able to think?" — pointing toward the consciousness that underlies and enables all sense-experience. Applied to the Karanas, the same question becomes: by what does the dancer dance? The Kena's answer — that it is Brahman, the universal consciousness — is the Karana tradition's deepest teaching: the 108 movements are not movements of a person but movements of the universal consciousness expressing itself through a particularly well-trained and surrendered human instrument.

The Taittiriya Upanishad's teaching on the five sheaths (pancha kosha) applies to the sequence of the 108 Karanas as a whole. The first quarter purifies the annamaya kosha (physical body). The second quarter transforms the pranamaya kosha (vital body). The third quarter opens the manomaya and vijnanamaya koshas. The fourth quarter dissolves the boundaries of the anandamaya kosha until the dancer rests directly in the Atman — the awareness in which all five bodies arise and dissolve.

Reference 3: Lalitha Sahasranamavali — Extended Tantric Synthesis

The Lalitha Sahasranamavali is not merely a devotional text but a complete Tantric cosmology encoded in divine names. Each name is a mantra, each mantra a portal into a specific dimension of divine reality. Reading the Sahasranamavali with the understanding of the Sri Chakra's geometry and the 108 Karanas' movement-vocabulary creates a tripartite synthesis: word, form, and movement united in a single act of total worship.

The name Nritya Priya (Name 167) — Lalitha who loves dance — is the explicit textual acknowledgment that the goddess herself is the supreme audience of the Karanas. The name Nrita Rupini (Name 168) — whose very form is dance — declares that the goddess is not merely a spectator but the dance itself. These two names together state the complete Tantric philosophy of the Karanas: Lalitha is both the audience (Nritya Priya) and the performance (Nrita Rupini), both the witness-consciousness (Purusha) and the dancing energy (Prakriti).

The name Pancha Yagnapriya (Name 233) — she who loves the five sacrifices — corresponds to the five-elemental structure of the Karanas: each of the five elements is a form of sacrifice in which the dense gives itself to the subtle. The 108 Karanas enact this five-fold sacrifice in movement: the dancer begins with earth-weight and arrives at ether-lightness, having offered each element of their being into the next.

The name Chidagni Kunda Sambhuta (Name 1) — born from the fire-pit of consciousness — is the most fundamental Tantric description of Lalitha's origin: she arises from the sacrificial fire of pure awareness. The first Karana, Sthānaka — the quiet, established standing posture — is this same origin: the stillness from which all movement arises, the silent fire of consciousness before its first movement into form. Between the first and the last, between Sthānaka and Urdhvajangha, the entire Lalitha Sahasranama is spoken in the language of the body. Every name of the goddess is a Karana; every Karana is a name of the goddess. This is the most complete expression of the Tantric vision: reality and its recognition, movement and its ground, the dance and the dancer, forever and inseparably One.
